# CRT Canon Chapter Template

Use this template for every Canon chapter.

A Canon chapter is not an essay, manifesto, or product document. It is a falsifiable research unit.

Each chapter should make a claim clear enough to test, limit, revise, or reject.

## 1. Question

What are we trying to understand?

Why does this question matter for trust, reciprocity, memory, identity, obligation, forgiveness, reputation, legitimacy, long-running agents, or HODLXXI?

## 2. Scope

What is included in this chapter?

What is excluded?

Which volume does this chapter belong to?

Which Lexicon terms does it rely on?

## 3. Observations

What can be seen directly?

Do not explain yet. Describe behavior, artifacts, incentives, records, scripts, receipts, failures, or human actions.

Separate observations into categories where useful:

- runtime behavior;
- human behavior;
- cryptographic evidence;
- economic incentives;
- historical or institutional patterns;
- failure cases.

## 4. Claim

What does this chapter claim?

State the claim plainly.

A good claim should be strong enough to matter and narrow enough to be wrong.

## 5. Non-claims

What does this chapter explicitly not claim?

This section is mandatory.

Use it to prevent overclaim, especially around:

- event proof vs obligation proof;
- key control vs identity;
- payment vs commitment;
- reputation telemetry vs social reputation;
- local memory vs durable public memory;
- capability vs legitimate authority;
- proof of funds vs trustworthiness.

## 6. Working theory

What mechanism might explain the observations?

What role is played by memory, incentives, status, identity, capital, signatures, constraints, legitimacy, forgiveness, or repeated interaction?

Why might the mechanism increase cooperation, accountability, continuity, or repair?

## 7. Runtime evidence

Which HODLXXI runtime mechanisms are relevant?

Examples may include:

- signed capabilities;
- paid jobs;
- Lightning invoices;
- receipts;
- attestations;
- `/agent/verify/<job_id>`;
- `/agent/reputation`;
- `/agent/chain/health`;
- Human Proof;
- operator continuity;
- covenant declarations;
- proof-of-funds surfaces;
- inter-agent messages;
- marketplace listing.

For each relevant mechanism, ask:

- What does it make visible?
- What does it not prove?
- What failure mode does it expose?
- What would make the mechanism stronger or weaker evidence?

## 8. Counterarguments

Why might this theory be false?

What would an adversary exploit?

What would a cynical human simulate?

What would a lazy user ignore?

What would a malicious agent imitate?

What would make the system look correct while failing in human meaning?

## 9. Historical analogies

Where has something similar appeared before?

Possible sources include kinship systems, tribes, churches, guilds, merchant networks, honor cultures, debt jubilees, banking, constitutions, common law, Bitcoin, online reputation systems, failed communes, failed protocols, and cartelized institutions.

Do not use analogy as proof. Use analogy to generate hypotheses and failure modes.

## 10. Failure modes

How could the claim fail in practice?

Consider:

- status theater;
- symbolic laundering;
- reputation fraud;
- sybil reset;
- cartel formation;
- revenge spirals;
- coercive dependency;
- overclaiming cryptographic proof;
- ritual behavior without real constraint;
- memory without repair;
- forgiveness without changed behavior;
- delegated authority without legitimacy.

## 11. Implications for HODLXXI

What should the system encode?

What should remain social?

What should be visible?

What should be private?

What should be reversible?

What should be forgiven?

What should be permanent?

What should be impossible?

What should not yet be built?

## 12. Falsification

What evidence would weaken or refute the chapter?

What measurement, runtime behavior, user behavior, or failure mode would show that the claim does not hold?

What observation would force Canon revision?

A strong chapter should name at least one concrete way it could be wrong.

## 13. Open questions

What remains unknown?

What should be measured?

What should be tested in runtime?

What needs historical research?

What needs simulation?

What should not yet be automated?
