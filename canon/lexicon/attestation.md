# Attestation

## Definition

An attestation is a signed or otherwise attributable statement about an event, actor, claim, or state.

In CRT, an attestation is evidence. It is not automatically truth, legitimacy, or final judgment.

## Operational meaning

An attestation becomes useful when it states:

- who or what made the statement;
- what is being claimed;
- what evidence supports the claim;
- when the statement was made;
- how it can be checked;
- what remains unproven.

## What an attestation is not

An attestation is not full reputation.

An attestation is not necessarily neutral.

An attestation is not necessarily true.

An attestation is not final authority.

An attestation is not repair.

## Observable behavior

In HODLXXI, attestations may appear as public records derived from jobs, receipts, event entries, or trust surfaces.

They can help external observers see remembered behavior without replaying all raw history.

## Failure modes

- Attestations become reputation laundering.
- False or partial attestations appear authoritative.
- Attestations are detached from context.
- A volume of attestations is mistaken for trustworthiness.
- No correction or counter-attestation path exists.

## Implications for HODLXXI

HODLXXI should eventually distinguish attestations from counter-attestations, disputes, corrections, revocations, and repairs.

An attestation surface should not imply final judgment unless the judgment process is explicit.

## Open questions

- Who may attest to what?
- How should attestations be challenged?
- Should attestations decay?
- What makes an attestation legitimate?
- How should counter-attestations be represented?
