# Verification

## Definition

Verification is the act of checking evidence against a defined rule.

Verification answers whether a specific claim passes a specific test. It does not decide every social, moral, or institutional meaning of the claim.

## Operational meaning

Verification requires:

- a claim;
- evidence;
- a verification rule;
- a verifier;
- a result;
- a boundary around what the result means.

## What verification is not

Verification is not trust by itself.

Verification is not satisfaction.

Verification is not legitimacy.

Verification is not final judgment.

Verification is not repair.

## Observable behavior

In HODLXXI, verification may appear through:

- receipt verification;
- signature checks;
- event-chain health checks;
- requester proof checks;
- proof-of-funds checks;
- covenant/script checks.

## Failure modes

- A verified artifact is treated as socially settled.
- Verification hides the narrowness of the underlying claim.
- Users trust the verifier without understanding the verification rule.
- A verifier checks syntax while users infer meaning.
- Verification succeeds but the human counterparty disputes interpretation.

## Implications for HODLXXI

Verification endpoints should expose claim boundaries.

A verifier should not silently convert narrow technical validity into broad social legitimacy.

## Open questions

- What should verification pages show to humans?
- Should verification results include explicit non-claims?
- How should failed verification be remembered?
- How should later correction or dispute affect a verified artifact?
